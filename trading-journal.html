I am trading the reaction of price to institutional footprints — specifically the points where the algorithm returns to collect orders (order blocks, FVGs) after creating displacement. My edge is the confluence of: HTF trend alignment + liquidity sweep + return to premium/discount + lower timeframe confirmation of a shift in delivery.

Entry Rules — What Must Be True to Enter
1.
HTF bias must be clear and aligned with the trade direction. If the higher timeframe is ranging or unclear, do not trade.
2.
Price must be at a defined Point of Interest (POI) — order block, FVG, supply/demand zone, or key S/R level. No POI, no trade.
3.
Liquidity must have been swept before the entry. Entering before a sweep means entering in the direction of the hunt — wrong side of the move.
4.
Lower timeframe must confirm a shift in delivery (CHoCH, BOS, or displacement candle) before entering. Do not anticipate — wait for confirmation.
5.
Minimum R:R of 1:2 must be available before entry. If TP target is blocked by a major level, the trade does not qualify.
6.
Risk per trade must not exceed [define your %]. Position size must be calculated before entry, not estimated.

01
Pre-Session Analysis — Before You Look for Trades
▶
1
Mark the HTF (H4 / D1) first
Before anything else, go to your highest relevant timeframe. Identify: the dominant trend direction, the last significant high and low, any major unmitigated order blocks or FVGs, and which side of the range price is currently on (premium or discount).
Why: Every lower timeframe setup must make sense in the context of HTF delivery. Trading against this is trading against the algorithm's current programme.
2
Identify key levels for the session
Mark: previous day high and low, previous week high and low, any obvious liquidity pools (equal highs/lows), significant round numbers, and your POIs (order blocks, FVGs, S/D zones) on H1.
Why: The algorithm seeks liquidity. Knowing where it sits tells you where price is likely to move toward before reversing.
3
Form your bias — and write it down
State clearly: "Today I am looking for BUYS / SELLS / no trade because [reason]." Log this in your pre-session analysis field. This creates accountability — you will later compare what you thought with what actually happened.
Why: Writing the bias before seeing intraday moves removes hindsight bias. It also forces clarity of thought before execution.

02
Setup Identification — Is There a Valid Trade?
▶
1
Is price at a defined POI?
Price must be interacting with a pre-identified level — not one you've drawn after the fact. The POI should be: an order block, FVG, supply/demand zone, or key S/R. If you're drawing levels after price has already moved to them, that is retrospective bias — not analysis.
Why: Chasing price away from a POI is one of the most common causes of poor entries and blown trades.
2
Has liquidity been swept?
Before a genuine reversal, the algorithm typically sweeps the obvious liquidity — the stop losses sitting above equal highs or below equal lows. Ask: has the sweep happened? Is price now rejecting from the POI after the sweep? If liquidity hasn't been taken, price may still be hunting — wait.
Why: Entries before a liquidity sweep often become the liquidity that gets swept. Patience here directly prevents avoidable losses.
3
Does lower timeframe confirm a shift?
Drop to your entry timeframe (M1 or M5). Look for: a CHoCH (price breaks a structural high/low in the opposite direction), a displacement candle (strong impulsive move), or a BOS. This is your trigger — not the POI itself. The POI tells you where. The LTF confirmation tells you when.
Why: POIs alone do not guarantee reversals. The LTF confirmation is the evidence the algorithm has changed its current delivery programme.
4
Calculate the R:R before entering
Identify: entry, SL (below/above the POI or swing), and TP (next significant level or liquidity pool). Calculate R:R. If it is below 1:2, the trade does not qualify. Check: is there any major S/R or opposing POI blocking the TP path? If yes, either adjust TP or skip the trade.
Why: A positive R:R means you can be wrong more often than right and still be profitable. Entries with poor R:R erode account even with a high win rate.

Liquidity has been swept before the entry
LTF confirmation of structural shift present
R:R calculated and is at least 1:2
No major S/R blocking the TP path
Setup aligns with HTF bias from pre-session
03
Entry Logging — What to Record at Entry
▶
1
Record all session and instrument data
Date, time, session (London open, NY open, kill zone etc.), instrument, analysis timeframe, entry timeframe. This is not optional — it becomes the data that shows you which sessions and instruments you trade best.
2
Select every confluence present — including opposing ones
Check every confluence that was present at entry — not just the ones that supported your trade. Mark the "against" confluences too. This data reveals whether trades with more opposing factors actually lose more often, which becomes a rule.
Why: Only logging supporting confluences creates a biased record. The full picture is what produces actionable insight.
3
Take a screenshot before entry
Capture the chart at the moment of entry or analysis — before price has moved. This is your "before" image. It shows your actual read, not a retrospective interpretation. Annotate clearly: mark your POI, SL, TP, and the liquidity you identified.
4
Write your pre-trade analysis
In 3–5 sentences: what you see, why you're entering, where you're wrong (SL), where you're right (TP), and what would make you exit early. Voice note works — append it to analysis. Write this before or immediately at entry, not after the trade closes.
5
Record your emotional state honestly
This is not to judge you — it is to identify patterns. Trades entered in FOMO or revenge consistently underperform. The data will show this. But only if you record it honestly.
04
Post-Trade Review — After the Trade Closes
▶
1
Record the result and P&L
Win, loss, break-even, or still open. Actual exit price and P&L in dollars. Do not approximate — exact figures only. This feeds your edge statistics.
2
Take a screenshot after close
Capture the chart showing the full trade from entry to exit. Your "after" image. Compare it against your "before" — did price follow your thesis? What did you miss?
3
Write the narrative of what happened
Describe exactly what price did — not what you wanted it to do. Then describe how you managed the trade. Then compare: was your pre-trade thesis correct? Did price deliver as you anticipated? If not — why not? What did the algorithm actually do?
Why: This narrative is where learning lives. Without it, you just have results. With it, you understand causation.
4
Extract one rule or lesson
Every trade must produce one explicit statement: what it confirmed, refuted, or revealed about your approach. Write it in the "Rule this entry validates or invalidates" field. Over time, these become your rulebook.
Why: Random logging produces random insight. Explicit rule extraction converts experience into structured knowledge.
5
Rate your rules adherence
Did you follow your rules? All of them? Which one did you break, if any? This is separate from whether you won or lost — a rules violation that wins is still a problem because it rewards bad behaviour. Log adherence independently of outcome.

05
Study Sessions — Setups You Watched But Didn't Take
▶
1
Why log setups you didn't take?
These are often the most instructive entries in your journal. A setup you observed, analysed, and correctly identified — but did not take — shows you that your analysis was right even when your execution was absent. Over time, this builds the confidence to pull the trigger when criteria are met.
Conversely: a setup you correctly avoided because criteria weren't met is equally valuable — it validates your patience.
2
What to record for study entries
Select "Study Session" or "Market Observation" as entry type. Record: instrument, session, HTF context, what setup appeared, what confluences were present, why you did or didn't take it, and what actually happened. Screenshot the setup and the outcome.
3
Backtesting sessions
When backtesting — scrolling back through historical price — log each potential setup using the same fields. Mark the entry type as "Study Session." Use the same confluence checklist, the same setup classification, and record what the outcome was. Backtesting data feeds the same edge statistics as live data.
Why: Consistent categorisation means all data — live, demo, and backtest — contributes to understanding your strategy's actual performance across conditions.
06
Weekly Review — Reading Your Own Data
▶
1
Go to the Edge tab every Sunday
Review: which confluences produced the highest win rate, which setups performed best, which sessions were most profitable, and what your emotional patterns show. This is not a feeling exercise — it is a data exercise. Let the numbers tell you the truth.
2
Update the Strategy Document
Based on the week's data, adjust one thing in your strategy. Not everything — one thing. Add a rule, refine a condition, or remove something you thought was important but the data doesn't support. Small, deliberate refinements compound over months into a precise edge.
3
Ask these questions every week
Which trades followed all my rules? Which didn't? Of the rule-following trades, what was the win rate? Are my losses coming from rule violations or from valid setups that simply didn't work? What was I most uncertain about this week?
This distinction — loss from violation vs. loss from valid setup — is critical. Only the former is fixable through discipline. The latter requires strategy refinement.
The goal is not a perfect win rate. The goal is a clear, repeatable edge that you understand deeply enough to trust — even through losing streaks — because you know exactly what conditions produce your wins.
